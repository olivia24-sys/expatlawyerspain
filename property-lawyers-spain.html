Property Lawyers in Spain

Verified English-speaking property lawyers for purchases, sales and disputes — independent of the agent, the developer and the notary.
Updated July 2026

Spain has no equivalent of UK conveyancing. The notario who witnesses your purchase works for the transaction, not for you — nobody in the standard Spanish process checks the property on the buyer's behalf unless you hire your own lawyer. For foreign buyers that check is the difference between a clean purchase and inheriting someone else's debts: unpaid IBI and community fees transfer with the property, not with the seller. The contrato de arras — where deals go wrong

Before completion you'll sign the contrato de arras — a private deposit contract between you and the seller, typically at 10% of the purchase price. It is legally binding and not standardised: if you pull out, you lose the deposit; if the seller pulls out, they owe you double. Terms, conditions and exit clauses are all negotiable — which means they're all checkable, and the moment to check them is before you sign, not after. A property lawyer reviews the arras as a standard part of the instruction.

What independent due diligence covers: title and ownership at the Land Registry, outstanding charges and embargoes, unpaid IBI and community fees (both transfer to you with the property), planning status and building licences, and — on new builds — the developer's bank guarantee on your stage payments.

Non-EU buyers: the 100% tax question

Spain's proposed 100% tax on non-EU property buyers made headlines in 2025 — and is regularly misreported. What has actually passed into law, who could be affected and what UK buyers should do now is covered in our dedicated guide to the non-EU property tax proposal. Short version: don't restructure a purchase around a proposal that hasn't passed; do have a lawyer confirm the current position before you commit a deposit.

Do you actually need a property lawyer in Spain?

Legally, no — Spain doesn't require buyers to use a lawyer, and that's exactly why so many foreign buyers get caught. Everyone else at the table (agent, developer, notary, seller's bank) is paid by the transaction going through, not by it going through safely.

In practice a foreign buyer should never complete without independent representation. It matters most when:

  • Buying off-plan — your stage payments must be secured by a bank guarantee; unsecured payments to a developer that fails are usually gone.
  • Buying rural or older property — planning irregularities and unregistered extensions are common, and legalising them after purchase is your problem, at your cost.
  • Buying from a non-resident seller — the 3% retention (Modelo 211) is your legal obligation as the buyer; get it wrong and the tax office pursues you.
  • Selling — a lawyer handles the capital gains position, the 3% retention refund and the Plusvalía calculation.

At roughly 1% of the purchase price, the lawyer is one of the smallest line items in the 10–14% costs stack — and the only one working for you. More detail: do I need a lawyer to buy property in Spain?

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does a property lawyer cost in Spain?
Around 1% of the purchase price, with typical minimum fees of €1,000–2,000 on lower-value properties. On a €300,000 purchase expect €2,500–3,500 all in for legal fees, covering due diligence through to completion.
Doesn’t the notary check the property for me?
No. The notario is a neutral public official who verifies identities and witnesses the deed — they do not check the property’s legal status on your behalf. Only a lawyer you hire independently does that.
Can these lawyers help if I’m buying from abroad?
Yes. Buying remotely via power of attorney is routine — your lawyer attends the notary on your behalf. Several listed firms handle the whole purchase for buyers who are not yet in Spain.
What taxes will I pay when buying?
On a resale: ITP at 6–10% depending on the region. On a new build: 10% IVA plus roughly 1.5% stamp duty. Budget 10–14% on top of the purchase price for all taxes, fees and legal costs combined.
