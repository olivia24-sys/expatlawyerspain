The visa routes an immigration lawyer handles in 2026

With the Golden Visa gone, two routes carry most expat moves to Spain:

VisaIncome requirement (2026)Work allowed?
Non-Lucrative Visa (NLV)€2,400/month passive income for a single applicant (400% of IPREM), plus €600/month per dependantNo — passive income only
Digital Nomad Visa (DNV)€2,850/month (200% of the minimum wage, updated January 2026), plus €1,069/month for a spouse and €356/month per childYes — remote work, with at most 20% of income from Spanish clients

The DNV also opens the door to the Beckham Law — a flat 24% income tax rate on Spanish-source income up to €600,000 for up to six years. The application (Modelo 149) must be made within six months of the start-of-activity date on your Spanish Social Security registration, and missing that window forfeits it permanently. A lawyer handling your visa will normally bundle the Beckham application into the same instruction.

Beyond the headline visas, immigration lawyers in Spain handle family reunification, the arraigo regularisation routes for people already in Spain, work permits, student-visa conversions, visa refusals and appeals, and Spanish nationality applications.

Residency paperwork: NIE, TIE and the queues

Every route ends in the same paperwork. Your NIE is the identification number Spain uses to track you financially — nothing of legal significance happens without it. The TIE is the physical residency card non-EU nationals collect once a visa is approved.

The bottleneck is appointments. Public NIE and TIE appointment slots in Barcelona and Madrid regularly run 6–8 weeks out; lawyers and gestores have access to appointment channels that aren't available through the public booking system and can often get the same thing done in days. If you're on a deadline — a property completion, a job start date, a visa expiry — that difference is usually the whole reason to instruct someone.

The 90-day rule

Without a visa, UK and US nationals can spend at most 90 days in any 180-day period in the Schengen area. Overstaying creates a record that complicates every later application — it's the single most common self-inflicted problem immigration lawyers are asked to fix.

What immigration lawyers charge in Spain

Immigration and residency work in Spain typically runs €500 to €2,000 as a fixed fee depending on visa type and complexity — a Digital Nomad Visa application with a Beckham Law election sits at the top of that range, a straightforward NLV renewal near the bottom. Get the fee agreement in writing before any work begins; a reputable firm will offer that without being asked.

When do you actually need an immigration lawyer?

If you're an EU citizen registering residency, or you need a one-off NIE for a bank account, you can usually manage the process yourself — it's form-filling plus patience with the appointment system.

A lawyer earns their fee in these situations:

  • Your application has any complication. A previous refusal, an overstay, a criminal-record certificate issue, or income that doesn't fit neatly into the passive/active boxes. These need to be presented correctly the first time — appeals take months.
  • You're combining a visa with tax planning. The Beckham Law's six-month deadline runs from your Social Security registration in Spain, not from when you get around to it. Visa and tax election need to be sequenced by one adviser.
  • You're on a hard deadline. Professional access to appointment slots is the difference between days and 6–8 weeks in the big cities.
  • Family reunification or nationality. Both are documentation-heavy and unforgiving of gaps; both are routinely refused on technicalities that a specialist would have caught.

How much does an immigration lawyer cost in Spain?
Most immigration and residency work is quoted as a fixed fee between €500 and €2,000 depending on the visa and complexity. A Digital Nomad Visa application with a Beckham Law tax election sits at the top of that range. Always get the fee agreement in writing before work begins.
Can I still get a Golden Visa in Spain?
No. Spain abolished the Golden Visa (residency by €500,000+ property investment) in April 2025. The main routes now are the Non-Lucrative Visa for people with passive income and the Digital Nomad Visa for remote workers.
